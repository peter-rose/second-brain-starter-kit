# Six Thinking Hats

Edward de Bono's framework for parallel thinking. Instead of arguing from fixed positions, every thinker wears the same hat at the same time and thinks in the same direction. The hats separate the modes. Facts stay separate from feelings. Risks stay separate from possibilities. Nothing bleeds into everything.

## How to use

**Full run**: "Six hats on [decision/idea/problem]" cycles through all six hats in sequence, ending with Blue Hat synthesis and a recommendation.

**Single hat**: "Black hat on this idea" or "Green hat only" runs one mode in depth when that's what the moment calls for.

**Quick scan**: "Quick hats on [topic]" gives one tight paragraph per hat, no elaboration.

**Decision variant**: "Hats on [Option A] vs [Option B]" runs Yellow + Black on each option, then Blue decides.

The Blue Hat always frames the question at the start and closes with the recommendation. The other five hats are the content.

## The Six Hats

---

### WHITE HAT — Facts & Information
*What do we actually know? What don't we know?*

**Mode**: Neutral. Objective. Information only. No interpretation, no opinion. Strip out everything that isn't a fact or a known unknown.

**What belongs here**:
- Data and facts that are confirmed
- Information gaps: what we need to know but don't
- What has already happened (history, outcomes, precedent)
- Numbers, timelines, evidence

**What doesn't belong here**: opinions disguised as facts, predictions without basis, interpretations of data, emotional reactions to data.

**The White Hat asks**:
- What do we know for certain?
- What's our source, and how reliable is it?
- What information do we need that we don't have?
- What has actually happened versus what we believe happened?
- Is this a fact or an assumption dressed as a fact?

**The discipline**: when White Hat thinking is on, challenge any statement that is actually an inference or belief. "Customers prefer X" is not White Hat. "In our last survey, 67% of respondents chose X over Y" is White Hat.

---

### RED HAT — Emotions & Intuition
*What does my gut say? What do I feel?*

**Mode**: Emotional. Subjective. Unapologetic. No justification required. The Red Hat gives feelings permission to be in the room without having to win an argument.

**What belongs here**:
- Gut reactions, positive and negative
- Intuitions about people, timing, fit
- What excites you about this
- What makes you uncomfortable even if you can't explain why
- Emotional temperature of the team, the stakeholders, the customer

**What doesn't belong here**: logic, data, explanations for why you feel the way you do. Under the Red Hat, feelings don't owe justification.

**The Red Hat asks**:
- What's my immediate gut reaction to this?
- What am I excited about, honestly?
- What am I uneasy about, honestly?
- What do I feel like I'm not saying in the room?
- What does my instinct say about this person, this timing, this decision?

**The discipline**: the Red Hat is not permission to vent. It is permission to surface the emotional information that usually gets suppressed in professional settings. The question is always: what real signal is in this feeling?

---

### BLACK HAT — Critical Judgment & Risk
*What could go wrong? What are the real risks?*

**Mode**: Cautious. Logical. Skeptical. The Black Hat is not pessimism. It is the discipline of identifying genuine risk before it becomes real. It is the most valuable hat for protecting against bad decisions.

**What belongs here**:
- Risks and downsides
- What has failed before and why
- Legal, ethical, compliance, or regulatory exposure
- Resource constraints and feasibility problems
- Weaknesses in the plan
- What assumptions are being made that could be wrong
- What could go wrong even if everything is done correctly

**What doesn't belong here**: emotions, opinions, personal preferences disguised as risks. Every Black Hat concern should be possible to explain logically.

**The Black Hat asks**:
- Why might this fail?
- What assumptions are we making that could be wrong?
- What's the worst realistic outcome?
- Where has this approach failed before, in this org or elsewhere?
- What are we not allowed to do that we might be assuming we can?
- What happens if the key person, resource, or assumption disappears?
- What is the cost of this decision being wrong?

**The discipline**: Black Hat is not about killing ideas. It is about making ideas stronger by forcing them to survive scrutiny. A plan that passes rigorous Black Hat thinking is a plan worth betting on.

---

### YELLOW HAT — Optimism & Value
*Why could this work? What's the best case?*

**Mode**: Constructive. Positive. Logical optimism, not cheerleading. The Yellow Hat finds the genuine value in an idea, the best realistic outcome, and the reasons it can work. Grounded, not wishful.

**What belongs here**:
- Benefits and upside
- Why this could work, with reasoning
- The best realistic outcome
- What value this creates, for the org, the customer, the team
- Why the timing is right
- What strengths you're building on

**What doesn't belong here**: blind optimism without basis. Yellow Hat optimism must be supportable. "This will work because X," not "this will work."

**The Yellow Hat asks**:
- What's the best realistic outcome here?
- Why could this work, with reasons rather than hope?
- What value does this create and for whom?
- What strengths and assets do we have that support this?
- What precedent or evidence suggests this approach succeeds?
- Even if parts of this fail, what value can be preserved?

**The discipline**: Yellow Hat thinking is often the least practiced. Under time pressure and organizational risk-aversion, people skip straight to Black Hat. The discipline is to genuinely explore the positive case before stress-testing it.

---

### GREEN HAT — Creativity & Alternatives
*What else could we do? What if we tried something different?*

**Mode**: Generative. Divergent. Lateral. Under the Green Hat there are no bad ideas, only unexplored possibilities. This is the hat for provocation, for "what if," for approaches that weren't on the original list.

**What belongs here**:
- Alternative approaches to the problem
- Creative reframes of the question itself
- "What if" scenarios and provocations
- New ideas, even half-formed ones
- Combinations of existing ideas
- Solutions borrowed from adjacent domains
- The thing nobody has said yet because it feels too obvious or too weird

**What doesn't belong here**: evaluation, criticism, feasibility assessment. Green Hat is generative. All judgment is suspended.

**The Green Hat asks**:
- What else could we do that we haven't tried?
- Is this the right question, or is there a better question underneath it?
- What would we do if the obvious constraint didn't exist?
- What would a completely different industry do with this problem?
- What's the opposite of what we're proposing? Could it work?
- What's one idea we've dismissed too quickly?
- What would we do if we started from scratch?

**The discipline**: Green Hat requires suspending evaluation long enough to generate something genuinely new. The instinct to immediately Black Hat a new idea kills creativity. The constraint: generate first, evaluate after.

---

### BLUE HAT — Process & Control
*What are we thinking about? What should we think about next?*

**Mode**: Meta. Orchestrating. The Blue Hat is about the thinking process, not the content. It frames the question at the start and synthesizes at the end. It is the facilitator hat.

**What belongs here at the start**:
- Defining the question clearly: what are we actually deciding?
- Identifying which hats matter most for this specific problem
- Setting the sequence and scope

**What belongs here at the end**:
- What did each hat surface?
- Where is there consensus?
- Where is there genuine tension that needs resolution?
- What is the recommendation?
- What's the next action?

**The Blue Hat asks**:
- What is the actual question we're trying to answer?
- Are we solving the right problem?
- What did we learn from each hat?
- What's the tension between the hats, and what does it tell us?
- What is the recommendation given everything we've surfaced?
- What's the decision and what's the next move?

**The discipline**: Blue Hat never argues for a specific outcome. It synthesizes. When the Blue Hat says "the recommendation is X," it is because the other five hats, taken together, point there.

---

## Output format

**BLUE HAT — Opening**
State the question clearly. Note which hats will be most load-bearing for this problem.

**WHITE HAT**
Facts confirmed. Information gaps. What we know vs. what we're assuming.

**RED HAT**
Gut reactions. What's exciting. What's uncomfortable. What's being left unsaid.

**BLACK HAT**
Risks. Weaknesses. What could fail. What assumptions are fragile.

**YELLOW HAT**
The real upside. Why it could work, with reasoning. What value it creates.

**GREEN HAT**
Alternatives. Reframes. What if. Lateral moves. The idea nobody's said yet.

**BLUE HAT — Synthesis**
What the hats collectively reveal. Where there's consensus and where there's tension. The recommendation. The next action.

---

## Rules

- Keep each hat in its lane. If something belongs in Black, don't let it bleed into White as a "fact." The discipline is the value.
- Black Hat and Yellow Hat must both be logical, not just emotional. The feeling goes in Red.
- Green Hat suspends evaluation entirely. No "but that won't work" until after the hat is finished.
- Blue Hat synthesis should surface tension between hats, not just summarize each one. The places where White and Red conflict, or where Yellow and Black are both strong, are the most useful signals.
- When in doubt about what to recommend: if Black Hat concerns can be mitigated and Yellow Hat value is real, move forward with conditions. If Black Hat surfaces something unmitigatable, stop.
